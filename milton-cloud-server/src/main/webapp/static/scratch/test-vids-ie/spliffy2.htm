Below are the three most authoritative definitions of intellectual disability (we will call it ID from now on)

  • http://www.aamr.org/content_100.cfm?n  avID=21 (American Association for Intellectual Disabilities)
  • See page 176 http://www.who.int/classifications/icd/en/bluebook.pdf (World Health Organisation ICD-10)
  • http://www.dsm5.org/ProposedRevisions/Pages/proposedrevision.aspx?rid=384 (Proposed DSM-5 revision of the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM-IV) definition)

These definitions all refer to a person having an ID if these three elements are present:

Below average intelligence – with an IQ of 70 or less, meaning at least two standard deviations below the mean.

What is intelligence?

  • Refers to general capacity to engage in cognitive functions such as learning, reasoning, manipulating information, identifying patterns and relationships, solving problems, recall, planning etc
  • Complex and multifaceted
  • Assessed using one of several standardised tests for which the average population score (“Intelligence Quotient” or “IQ”) is 100, and one standard deviation is 15 points
Limitations of IQ Testing

What does IQ actually measure? What does IQ fail to measure? How much more intelligent is someone for example who scores 70 than someone who scores 75?

It is very easy to invest a whole lot of meaning in someone’s IQ – it’s such a nice simple number! But it is important to bear in mind that it is in fact a score calculated from a person’s responses to a series of questions and tasks.

What influences a person’s answers to any question?

Obviously this is complex – motivation, ability, alertness, level of comfort, concentration level, prior exposure/familiarity, and so on. Note that most of these vary significantly over time.

The psychologists who developed intelligence tests, and the psychologists administering the intelligence tests, are aware of all these factors. Therefore the writers and administrators of the tests do everything they can to make sure that factors that are potentially very variable and are not a part of intelligence do not affect an individual’s performance on a test.

But conditions are so rarely perfect, and all humans are so complex, that it would be naive to believe that a person’s answers to the test are ever only influenced by their actual intelligence.

What is “intelligence”?

This is something psychologists have been debating and exploring for a long time, and it is generally agreed that intelligence is complex and involves many different kinds of functioning.

IQ testing groups tasks into different ‘domains’ of intelligence.  Test performance can vary substantially between domains, so a person can have strengths and weaknesses that are not apparent from just a single IQ score.

The take home message is: IQ can be useful as an approximate global indicator, but it is unwise to equate it rigidly with a person’s actual intelligence, or as a direct indicator of a person’s abilities.

 

IQ score is used to roughly categorise a person’s level of ID – from borderline ID through to profound ID. You will learn more on this in the next section.

 

PLUS deficits in adaptive functioning

What is adaptive functioning?

  • Refers to age-appropriate behaviours necessary for people to live independently and to function safely and appropriately in daily life
  • Involves different types of skills
    • conceptual skills—language and literacy; money, time, and number concepts; self-direction 1
    • social skills—interpersonal skills, social responsibility, self-esteem, gullibility, naïveté, social problem solving, the ability to follow rules/obey laws and to avoid being victimised. 1
    • practical skills— “Skills of daily living” such as personal care, occupational skills, healthcare, travel/transportation, schedules/routines, safety, use of money, use of the telephone. 1
    • communication skills 2  – the ability to express self (verbally and non-verbally) in ways that others can understand, and/or the ability to understand what others express (verbally and non-verbally)
  • is assessed using one of several standardised tests e.g. Vineland Adaptive Behaviour Scales; Scales of Independent Behaviour – Revised; Adaptive Behaviour Scales; Supports Intensity Scale; etc  
 
Consider the complexity of adaptive functioning

Just like intelligence, adaptive functioning refers to many different kinds of ability.

Tests of adaptive functioning rely only on the behaviours and abilities that are explicit in the questions. No set of questions will ever cover all potential areas or mechanisms of functional behaviour. Therefore, while scores on adaptive functioning tests and subscales give valuable indicators of a person’s functioning, there can always be aspects of an individual’s abilities, strengths and weaknesses that are not captured. Therefore it’s important to seek to get to know the individual beyond their test scores.

 

AND onset during the developmental period – before the age of 18

If the onset of deficits in adaptive functioning and/or below average IQ occur after the age of 18, this is usually due to a trauma to the brain (referred to as acquired brain injury) or a neurodegenerative disease (referred to as dementia).

Key Learning Point

ID involves more than just below average IQ. ID means a person also experiences difficulties in various domains of functioning, and has begun to experience these characteristics before the age of 18.

ID is a complex disability that can affect a person in many different ways, and specific patterns of ability and disability vary widely between individuals with ID.
